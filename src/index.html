About Alisa Balayan

>

Born into a family of ballet dancers it was no surprise that Alisa showed an early, prodigious talent for ballet. Dancing from age 3, the future professional dancer was consumed by dance.

Studying at the Lviv School of Ballet exposed her to some of the best teachers in Europe. Her teaching career began while still studying dance in Lviv. After a perfect score in her final exam she won a full scholarship to Joffrey Ballet school in New York. Studying with some of the best teachers in the world was a life changing experience.

Having received a BA degree in dance, from the Lviv State College of Arts and Culture, Alisa furthered her dance studies in Jazz-Modern and Contemporary as well as Ballet. Returning to Lviv to dance professionally with the Lviv State Ballet, Alisa resumed teaching, at the State Ballet School- teaching Jazz-Modern, Contemporary and Ballet.

Her career as a professional ballet dancer with the Lviv State Ballet included, Swan Lake, The Nutcracker Suite, Don Quixote, La Bayadere, & Sleeping Beauty amongst others.

Upon arriving in South Africa Alisa joined Mzansi dance company and also began teaching. She then became the Artistic Director of the Russian School of Ballet.

In 2019 Alisa started her own school, naming it for her maternal grandmother, the Prima Ballerina, Ludmilla Orlovska.

Orlovska History

>

Alisa’s grandmother Ludmila, was the Prima Ballerina at the Lviv National Opera and Ballet House. She trained at the Kiev State Choreographic Academy which was merged with Kiev Conservatory, during World War Two. Ludmila studied ballet (Vaganova method, Russian Ballet), character and historical dance. She was taught by Agrippina Vaganova’s famous graduate – Klavdia Vasina. An impressive career followed, including the lead roles in: Giselle (Giselle), Swan Lake (Odette), Spartacus (Phrygia), Romeo and Juliet (Juliet), Nutcracker (Clara), Sleeping Beauty (Aurora), Peer Gynt (Solveig), The Red Poppy (Tai-Choa), The Tale of the Stone Flower (Katherine), Les Sylphides, Grand Waltz and many others. After retiring at 35 Ludmilla began her career as a teacher, teaching for 49 years- including her granddaughter, Alisa.

Lumilla’s daughter Olena, followed her mother into ballet. Her dancing education started at age 5 at the State Choreographic School in Ukraine where she studied ballet, modern and character dance. She graduated from the State Choreographic School at the age of 16 with distinction.

In 1985, she completed her degree in ballet (Russian Ballet method -Vaganova syllabus), character and modern dance, with distinction at the Lviv College of Art and Culture. Her education continued at the Departments of Dance at the Ivan Franko National University of Lviv. Olena became the head ballet-master and choreographer at the Lviv National Drama Theater in 2006, where she has choreographed more than 50 major shows in Ukraine.

Upon arriving in South Africa Alisa joined Mzansi dance company and also began teaching. She then became the Artistic Director of the Russian School of Ballet.

Olena began her teaching career at the age of 17 when she started working with pre-ballet groups at the State Choreographic School. She continued teaching ballet, modern and character dance at the State Choreographic School until 2006. From 1992 to 2006 she owned and ran the Jazz and Modern Dance Company. Olena’s 37 years of experience as a dance teacher and choreographer are a great and constant inspiration to her daughter, Alisa.

Philosophy

>

The Vaganova method demands a strong technical foundation. technically correct movement is the starting point for losing oneself in the dance. Technique and joy and are equally important. Without the ability to express the joy of the movement, technique is meaningless. For dancers who prefer to dance for fun or exercise, her focus is to give them the technical grounding and expertise so that they can enjoy movement and express themselves through the dance. Alisa’s dream is to create a wonderful environment for both vocational and recreational dancers where their personalities and talents flourish. Dance as a tool for self-development is something Alisa is passionate about. Watching her students of all ages flourish is the ultimate reward. To help build confidence, skill, co-ordination, and self-expression, a nurturing body-positive environment is essential. Incorporating fun into classes is also important.

Dance Goals:

  1. Professional technique in ballet (Russian method, Contemporary and Jazz)
  2. Dance at competitions, festivals, & shows
  3. Pass exams in ballet (Vaganova method)
  4. Attainment of a career in dance
  5. Self-expression
  6. Joy & fun

Life Goals:

  1. Self confidence
  2. Discipline
  3. Ability to work in a team
  4. Good body conditioning
  5. Ability for creative thinking

Exams and Grading

>

Exams are not compulsory but highly recommended. Exams help to build student confidence, and they are assessed by an independent professional teacher, resulting in important feedback and training recommendations.

Exams also serve as a great motivational tool for students to work harder on their class routines.
The examiner’s mark decides whether the student has passed the level and can advance or if they should stay in the same level.

If a student does not participate in the examination, his/her regular teacher will decide whether the student will advance or not.

Vaganova Method

>

The Vaganova Method is a ballet technique and training system devised by the Russian dancer and pedagogue Agrippina Vaganova (1879–1951). While there is debate over which system is the best, simply put most of the worlds best dancers of the last and current century were trained in the Vaganova method. A good dancer can come come out of any system and no ballet company hires only Vaganova or RAD, or Cecchetti graduates.

Vaganova’s key strengths are; teaching correct movement and posture first, strict technique based on correct movement and very clear grading and marking systems. Many RAD & Cecchetti dancers need to unlearn or improve their technique upon entering the professional dance world.

Vaganova graduates are arguably more ready for the rigours of professional dancing.

From a parent’s perspective Vaganova may seem the better choice as the risk of injury is lower and assessments of the student’s progress are more accurate as the balance between technical ability and expression is more objectively obvious.

For dancers starting at a later age ( 5-7 years of age is normal) Vaganova method is a good choice as they will be immersed in correct technique allowing their natural ability to flourish faster.

Recreational dancers who dance purely for fun or exercise will benefit from a system of movement that reduces injury risk and quickly benefits endurance, posture and strength.
